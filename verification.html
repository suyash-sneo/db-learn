Verification & review workflow

This curriculum is written to be checkable. Every precise factual claim carries an inline [src: …] pointer, and anything reconstructed from memory wears a ⚠️VERIFY tag. This page is your checklist.

Why this exists

AI (including the AI that wrote these pages) can state a wrong byte offset or lock-state name with total confidence. Rather than pretend that never happens, the curriculum surfaces it: claims that matter and might be wrong are tagged so you can confirm them against the primary source before you rely on them in code.

Your ChatGPT review pass (recommended workflow)

  1. Work the tag list below. For each ⚠️VERIFY item, open the cited primary source (the exact SQLite doc section, source file, or repo).
  2. Ask a second model to cross-check. Paste the lesson's claim and the primary-source text into ChatGPT (or another model) and ask: "Does the source confirm this exact claim? Quote the line that does, or say it doesn't." Make it cite, not opine.
  3. Prefer the source over either model. If the doc and the lesson disagree, the doc wins. Note the correction.
  4. Record the result in the "status" column of your own copy, or as a margin note. A confirmed claim graduates from ⚠️VERIFY to trusted.
Good prompt for the cross-check

"Here is a claim from my study notes: <claim>. Here is the primary source text: <pasted doc/section>. Quote the exact sentence(s) that confirm or contradict the claim. If the source doesn't address it, say so — do not guess."

The two kinds of tags

  • [src: SQLite fileformat2.html §1.3] — a confirmed pointer. The claim was checked against this location while writing. You can still re-verify.
  • ⚠️VERIFY — reconstructed from memory or not fetched at authoring time. Treat as unconfirmed. Especially: source-file line numbers, function names, lecture timestamps, exact field sizes in formats not yet fetched, and the current state of fast-moving repos (Isar v4, community forks).
