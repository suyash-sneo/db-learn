3.6 — Delete, merge, rebalance

Goal: Understand the deletion choices—simple removal, redistribution, merge, and root shrink—without pretending that every byte-oriented B+tree uses one universal minimum-occupancy rule.

Why this matters to your DB

Deletion changes more than one leaf entry when it also changes a child's smallest key, empties a page, or triggers a space-utilization policy. A tree can remain search-correct with sparse pages if separators and links remain correct, but its space use and worst-case height bound become weaker. This lesson gives you the full design vocabulary before you choose an implementation policy.

The occupancy invariant

In a textbook fixed-order B-tree/B+tree, the definition includes a minimum number of children/entries for every non-root node, commonly around half of maximum capacity. That lower bound is part of the proof that height remains logarithmic. A byte-oriented page with variable cells does not have one natural “order”: ten tiny cells and ten large cells use different space. Its format must define a policy explicitly—for example, minimum encoded bytes, minimum entry count, or lazy cleanup. Search correctness depends on valid ordering and separators; utilization guarantees depend on the chosen policy.

The simple case: leaf still has enough

Descend to the leaf and remove the entry. If the leaf remains acceptable under your chosen policy, no page count changes. You may still need to update an ancestor separator when the removed key was the first key of a non-leftmost leaf.

Underflow: borrow, then merge

If the policy classifies the node as underfull, two standard repairs are:

  1. Redistribute with an adjacent sibling: move one or more boundary entries so both pages satisfy the policy, then derive the parent separator from the new right-child minimum. Variable-size cells may require moving more than one entry.
  2. Merge when the encoded contents of two siblings fit in one page: combine them, repair the leaf link if these are leaves, return the unused page to free-space management, and remove the corresponding separator and child pointer from the parent.

The cascade and shrinking the tree

Removing a separator from a parent may make the parent underfull, so repair can cascade upward. If an internal root ends with one child and no separators, that child can become the new root and the old root page can be freed. This is the structural inverse of creating a new root after a split.

Many real databases simplify deletion

Production policies vary. In SQLite 3.53.2, sqlite3BtreeDelete participates in the same balancing machinery used after other page changes; balance_nonroot redistributes cells among sibling pages and can change the number of pages. Freed database pages are managed separately from reducing the file's high-water mark. [src: pinned SQLite 3.53.2 sqlite3BtreeDelete, balance_nonroot, and balance dispatcher in src/btree.c] That is a source-study reference, not a requirement to reproduce SQLite's algorithm.

Build scope: deletion is deliberately deferred

The Phase 3 build requires persistent search, insert, split, duplicate-key replacement, and range scan. It does not require full B+tree deletion. Implementing delete before the node codec, split propagation, persistence, and separator convention are stable multiplies the debugging surface. Keep this lesson as the specification vocabulary for a later deletion milestone.

⚠️ Keep the separator and the boundary in sync

After borrowing or merging, the parent's separator key must still correctly describe the boundary between its children. A borrow that updates the leaf but not the separator (or vice versa) produces a tree that searches incorrectly without crashing — the worst kind of bug. Re-derive the separator from the children after every redistribution.

✅ Concept check

1. Why is “half full” not a complete rule for this variable-cell format?

Show answer
Half could mean entries or encoded bytes, and variable-size cells make those different. The format must define its occupancy policy before underflow can be detected.

2. In what order do you try to fix an under-full node?

Show answer
First borrow/redistribute from a sibling with spare encoded capacity; if two siblings fit in one page, merge is another option.

3. How does a B+tree shrink in height?

Show answer
When the root internal node is reduced to a single child, that child becomes the new root.

4. Why must you re-derive the parent separator after a borrow/merge?

Show answer
The separator must match the new boundary between children; a stale separator silently misroutes searches.
