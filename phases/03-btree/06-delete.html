3.6 — Delete, merge, rebalance

Goal: Remove a key from a leaf, and when a node falls below its minimum occupancy, restore the invariant by borrowing from a sibling or merging two nodes — the mirror image of splitting.

Why this matters to your DB

Deletion is the operation people most often get wrong or skip. A naive delete leaves nodes too empty, degrading fanout and eventually breaking the tree's guarantees. To keep lookups shallow under churn, you must rebalance on delete just as you split on insert.

The occupancy invariant

A B+tree keeps every node (except the root) at least half full — this lower bound is what guarantees the tree's logarithmic height. Insert maintains it by splitting full nodes; delete maintains it by fixing under-full nodes. The minimum is usually expressed as ⌈order/2⌉ entries; with variable-length entries it's often a byte threshold (e.g. a node may not drop below a quarter or half of usable bytes). ⚠️VERIFY the exact threshold you choose and apply it consistently.

The simple case: leaf still has enough

Descend to the leaf, remove the entry. If the leaf is still at or above minimum occupancy, you're done — no structural change. As with insert, most deletes are this simple.

Underflow: borrow, then merge

If removing leaves the node below minimum, restore the invariant in this order:

  1. Borrow (redistribute) from an adjacent sibling that has more than the minimum: move one entry from the rich sibling into the poor node, and update the parent's separator to match the new boundary. This fixes the underflow without changing the number of nodes.
  2. Merge if neither sibling can spare an entry: combine the under-full node with a sibling into one node (they jointly fit, since both were near the minimum), and remove the now-unused separator and child pointer from the parent.

The cascade and shrinking the tree

Removing a separator from a parent may push the parent below minimum, so borrow/merge can cascade upward exactly as splits cascade in insert. The terminal case: if the root is an internal node that ends up with a single child (no separators left), delete the root and make that single child the new root — the tree shrinks by one level. This is the only way a B+tree decreases in height, mirroring how root splits are the only way it grows.

Many real databases simplify deletion

Full borrow/merge is the textbook algorithm, but production systems often relax it. Some never merge eagerly — they let nodes get sparse and reclaim space lazily (vacuum/compaction). SQLite rebalances on delete via its balance routine but leaves freed space to be reused rather than always shrinking the file. ⚠️VERIFY SQLite's exact delete/balance behavior in btree.c before asserting specifics. For your build, implementing borrow and merge teaches the real invariant; a simpler "delete and tolerate sparse nodes" variant is an acceptable, clearly-documented fallback if time-boxed.

⚠️ Keep the separator and the boundary in sync

After borrowing or merging, the parent's separator key must still correctly describe the boundary between its children. A borrow that updates the leaf but not the separator (or vice versa) produces a tree that searches incorrectly without crashing — the worst kind of bug. Re-derive the separator from the children after every redistribution.

✅ Concept check

1. What invariant guarantees the tree stays shallow under deletion?

Show answer
Every non-root node stays at least ~half full (minimum occupancy), preserving logarithmic height.

2. In what order do you try to fix an under-full node?

Show answer
First borrow/redistribute from a sibling with spare entries; if none can spare one, merge with a sibling.

3. How does a B+tree shrink in height?

Show answer
When the root internal node is reduced to a single child, that child becomes the new root.

4. Why must you re-derive the parent separator after a borrow/merge?

Show answer
The separator must match the new boundary between children; a stale separator silently misroutes searches.
