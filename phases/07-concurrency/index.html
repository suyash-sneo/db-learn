Phase 7

Concurrency

Phase 5 gave you the A and D of ACID. This phase is about the I: what two or more transactions are allowed to observe about each other. You'll see why uncontrolled concurrent access corrupts data, study exclusion locks, snapshots, and multi-version concurrency control (MVCC) without treating them as mutually exclusive, then map them onto the tools you actually have: Rust's ownership-based concurrency and Dart's isolate model across the FFI boundary. The build is deliberately modest: an owner-aware, in-process lock policy for one engine handle, with explicit cross-process and snapshot limitations.

Why concurrency comes after durability

Isolation can be specified independently of crash durability, but implementing a usable engine requires the layers to agree on transaction boundaries. Putting concurrency after Phase 5 lets you define lock scope around a commit/rollback protocol you already understand: acquire before the first protected read or mutation, publish or roll back, then release.

What you'll be able to do after Phase 7

Name the concrete anomalies uncontrolled concurrency causes; explain SQLite's five rollback-journal lock states and how WAL mode changes the reader/writer story; describe the SQL isolation levels and distinguish tuple MVCC from SQLite's WAL page snapshots; choose a Rust synchronization primitive for a shared handle and justify it with Send/Sync; reason about calling your engine from a long-lived Dart worker isolate across the FFI; and implement a single-writer lock policy whose deterministic and threaded tests are honest about its limits.
